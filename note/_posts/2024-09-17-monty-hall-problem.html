--- title: "Monty Hall Problem" layout: post ---
  • Monty Hall Problem
    • Behind 3 doors, there is one car and two goats
    • When the contestant picks a door, the host (Monty Hall) opens one of the remaining doors to reveal a goat
    • The contestant then decides whether to stick with their original choice or switch to the other remaining door

    • Event definitions
      • \(A_1\): The car is behind door 1 (the door the contestant chose)
      • \(A_2\): The car is behind door 2
      • \(A_3\): The car is behind door 3
      • \(B\): The host opens door 2 to reveal a goat

    • Prior
      • \(P(A_1) = P(A_2) = P(A_3) = \frac{1}{3}\)

    • Likelihood
      • \(P(B|A_1) = \frac{1}{2}\): If the car is behind door 1, the host randomly chooses between door 2 or 3 (\(\frac{1}{2}\) each)
      • \(P(B|A_2) = 0\): If the car is behind door 2, the host cannot open door 2
      • \(P(B|A_3) = 1\): If the car is behind door 3, the host must open door 2

    • What are \(P(A_1|B)\) and \(P(A_3|B)\)?

    • Total probability \(P(B)\) \[ \begin{aligned} B &= (B \cap A_1) \cup (B \cap A_2) \cup (B \cap A_3) \\ \\ P(B) &= P(B \cap A_1) + P(B \cap A_2) + P(B \cap A_3) \\ \,\\ &= P(B|A_1)P(A_1) + P(B|A_2)P(A_2) + P(B|A_3)P(A_3) \\ \,\\ &= \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{3} + 0 \times \frac{1}{3} + 1 \times \frac{1}{3} \\ \,\\ &= \frac{1}{6} + 0 + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{2} \end{aligned} \]
    • Posterior
      • \[ \begin{aligned} P(A_1|B) &= \frac{P(B|A_1)P(A_1)}{P(B)} = \frac{\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{3}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{\frac{1}{6}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{3} \\\\ P(A_3|B) &= \frac{P(B|A_3)P(A_3)}{P(B)} = \frac{1 \times \frac{1}{3}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{\frac{1}{3}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{2}{3} \end{aligned} \]
    • Conclusion: Switching is advantageous
      • Probability of winning the car by staying with door 1: \(P(A_1|B) = \frac{1}{3}\)
      • Probability of winning the car by switching to door 3: \(P(A_3|B) = \frac{2}{3}\)
      • The host opening a door with a goat serves as new evidence (\(B\)) that updates the prior probability. Switching doubles the probability of winning
